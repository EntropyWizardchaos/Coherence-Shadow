# C↔M↔D Framework — Brief Context

*This document provides theoretical background for the coherence–dark matter test. Reading it is not required to replicate the empirical results.*

---

## The Framework

C↔M↔D (Coherence–Memory–Dimension) proposes that three quantities form a coupled dynamical system:

- **C** (Coherence): Organizational order; the degree to which a system maintains structured relationships
- **M** (Memory): Accumulated pattern; persistent information across time
- **D** (Dimension): Available degrees of freedom; geometric complexity

The core equation:

```
C = M / D
```

Coherence emerges when memory density exceeds dimensional dilution.

---

## Dark Matter Prediction

In this framework, "dark matter" is reinterpreted as **unresolved geometric slack** — dimensional structure that has not been organized into coherent patterns.

The prediction follows:

1. D naturally decays toward lower values (dimensional flattening)
2. Coherence (C) organizes dimensional slack into structure
3. Regions with higher coherence should have less "leftover" unstructured geometry
4. This leftover geometry manifests as what we measure as dark matter

Therefore: **Higher coherence → Lower dark matter fraction**

---

## Why Galaxies?

Galaxies are natural test cases because:

1. Dark matter fraction is measurable via rotation curves
2. Coherence has physical proxies:
   - Kinematic: smooth rotation = coherent orbits
   - Morphological: concentrated light = organized structure
3. Large samples exist (SPARC: 175 galaxies)

## Relationship to Standard Physics

This framework does not reject standard physics. It reframes the *interpretation* of dark matter:

- **Standard view:** Dark matter is an unknown particle with gravitational effects
- **C↔M↔D view:** Dark matter is unresolved geometric structure (no new particles required)

The rotation curves, mass decompositions, and observations remain identical. Only the ontological interpretation differs.

---

## What This Test Does NOT Prove

- That dark matter is "really" geometric slack
- That the framework is correct
- That standard dark matter models are wrong

**What it shows:** A predicted correlation exists in the data. The prediction was made before the test. The result is statistically significant and replicable.
